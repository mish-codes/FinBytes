Fast answers to a handful of risk / regulatory interview questions, each with a plain ELI10 version underneath.

Historical simulation vs Monte Carlo VaR — when each?

Historical simulation (HS) replays actual past risk-factor moves (say the last 250–500 days) on today's portfolio, sorts the resulting P&L, and reads off the percentile (e.g. the 99% loss). No distributional assumption; it captures the real fat tails and correlations exactly as they happened. Weaknesses: it can only produce scenarios that occurred in the window, reacts slowly, and assumes recent history is representative.

Monte Carlo (MC) assumes a model for the risk factors (distributions, vols, correlations), simulates thousands of random scenarios, revalues the book in each, and reads the percentile. It's flexible — handles heavy optionality / non-linearity, forward-looking vols, and tail scenarios history never showed — but it's model-dependent (garbage in, garbage out) and compute-heavy.

  • Use HS for simplicity, transparency, regulator-friendliness, and largely linear books where recent history is trusted.
  • Use MC for non-linear/optional portfolios, path-dependent products, forward-looking or custom distributions, and generating tail scenarios beyond the historical record.
ELI10: HS = "what would happen to my bag of stuff if the last two years of weather happened again, day by day?" MC = "let a dice-rolling machine invent thousands of made-up but believable weather days (using rules I set) and see how my stuff copes." Real history vs a rules-based imagination machine.

Why does FRTB move from VaR to Expected Shortfall?

VaR at 99% tells you the loss you won't exceed on 99% of days — but it says nothing about how bad the other 1% gets. A £100 VaR is the same whether the tail loss is £150 or £10,000. VaR is also not coherent (not sub-additive): it can occasionally punish diversification. Expected Shortfall (ES) — FRTB uses ES at 97.5% — is the average loss across the worst tail beyond the threshold: "given we breach, how bad on average?" It captures tail severity and is coherent. FRTB layers on liquidity horizons and stressed calibration on top.

ELI10: VaR says "I probably won't lose more than £100 on a bad day" — but it won't tell you if a really bad day costs £150 or £1,000. ES asks "on the genuinely bad days, how much do I lose on average?" — it looks past the line into the tail, so a few monster losses can't hide behind the threshold.

What does bitemporal data let a risk system do that a normal database can't?

Bitemporal data keeps two time axes: valid time (when a fact was true in the real world) and transaction/system time (when the system actually recorded it). That lets you ask two different questions: "what was the position/curve for last Tuesday?" and "what did we believe it to be as of last Tuesday when we ran the report?" Benefits:

  • Reproduce past reports exactly — rerun last week's VaR and get last week's number, even after corrections (audit / regulatory replay).
  • Separate real market moves from data fixes — late trades, back-dated amendments, and restatements are recorded without destroying what was known before.
  • As-of / as-at queries and clean P&L explain ("how much of the change is new market vs newly-learned info?").

A normal single-timeline database overwrites, so you lose what you knew at the time — you can't faithfully reproduce or defend a historical output.

ELI10: A normal database is a whiteboard — fix a mistake and you rub out the old number, so nobody can tell what you thought yesterday. Bitemporal is a diary in pen: it keeps both "what really happened on Monday" and "what I believed about Monday when I wrote it on Tuesday," so you can always rewind to exactly what you knew at any moment — perfect for audits.

CCAR vs FDSF — one line each

  • CCAR (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review) — the US Federal Reserve's annual stress test and capital-plan review checking that large US banks hold enough capital to survive a severe scenario.
  • FDSF (Firm Data Submission Framework) — the Bank of England / PRA's standardised UK data-submission framework that feeds its concurrent stress tests.
ELI10: CCAR = the American exam where the Fed checks a big bank could survive a money storm and still have cash left. FDSF = the British "fill in this giant spreadsheet" rules, so the Bank of England has the data to run its own storm-survival check.
