A fast sanity-check on the products and the risk language for a commodities risk / risk-systems conversation. The recurring theme: a commodity isn't a single price — it's a forward curve, so almost everything (delta, vol, P&L) is bucketed along that curve rather than collapsed to one number.

ELI10 — the lemonade-stand version

Imagine you run a lemonade stand and you've promised to buy lemons every month for the next year. The price of lemons isn't one number — it's a different price for each month (cheap in summer, dear in winter). That list of monthly prices is the forward curve, and it's the heart of everything.

  • Delta — if lemon prices nudge up, how much do you gain or lose? And because every month has its own price, you track each month separately — that's "bucketed along the curve."
  • Gamma — how quickly that gain/loss changes: gentle slope, or about to fall off a cliff?
  • Vega — you also care how jumpy prices are, not just where they are. A calm market vs a wild one. Vega is how much a wilder market changes the value of your promises.
  • Theta — options are like ice cubes: they melt a little every day that passes.

The deals: sometimes you lock a price now (a future/forward); sometimes you buy the right but not the obligation to buy (an option); sometimes you settle on the average price over the whole month instead of one day (an Asian option — common for oil cargoes); sometimes you can choose how many lemons to take within limits (a swing deal); sometimes you keep lemons in the fridge to sell later (storage); and sometimes you bet on the gap between two prices — like lemonade minus lemons (a spread; in power, electricity minus gas is the spark spread).

Why it's hard for the computers: there are many months, many "fruits" (gas, power, oil…), and risk teams keep asking "what if?" thousands of times (what if a heatwave? a frost?). Each question redoes the maths for every month and every fruit — so you end up with a mountain of numbers to crunch and store, fast.

Greeks — the commodities flavour

ELI10

Say you have a coupon that lets you buy a chocolate bar next month for £1 — whatever it actually costs by then. That coupon is an option. The Greeks just describe how the coupon's worth changes as things move:

  • Delta — does the coupon gain when chocolate gets pricier? If chocolate rises £1.00 → £1.10, your "buy at £1" coupon is worth more. Delta ≈ how many pennies the coupon gains per penny chocolate rises. A coupon that's already a steal moves almost penny-for-penny (delta near 1); one that looks useless barely reacts (delta near 0).
  • Gamma — how fast does that reaction grow? As chocolate climbs toward £1, the coupon suddenly starts reacting much more strongly (delta 0.2 → 0.5 → 0.8). Gamma is the speed of that change — biggest right around the £1 mark.
  • Theta — the coupon expires. Even if chocolate's price sits perfectly still, the coupon is worth a little less each day, because there's less time left for a price jump to help you. That daily bleed is theta.
  • Vega — how wild do people expect things to get? If everyone suddenly expects chocolate prices to swing wildly before the deadline, your coupon is worth more (better chance it leaps past £1). Vega is the gain when expected wildness (volatility) rises — even with today's price unchanged.
  • Rho — interest rates give a tiny nudge; usually just a footnote here.

The commodities twist: you don't hold one coupon — you hold a coupon for each delivery month, so you track delta and vega for every month separately (and winter behaves differently from summer).

And the same four, precisely:

  • Delta — sensitivity to the underlying forward price. Crucially, in commodities delta is usually bucketed along the forward curve (a sensitivity to each tenor) rather than one figure. Seasonality matters — gas and power have strong seasonal shape, so winter and summer tenors move differently and are risk-managed separately.
  • Gamma — convexity; how delta changes as the forward moves. Again, per-bucket.
  • Vega — sensitivity to volatility, read off a vol surface by tenor and strike (smile/skew), not a single vol.
  • Theta — time decay.
  • Rho / rate sensitivity — usually secondary in commodities (discounting matters, but the curve and vol are the story).

Products

ELI10

Same chocolate shop — these are the different deals you can strike:

  • Future / forward = a locked-in promise. "I'll buy 10 bars next month at a price we fix today." You're committed — no choice later, but no nasty surprise either.
  • Swap = swap a bouncy bill for a steady one. Instead of paying whatever chocolate costs each month (up and down), you agree to always pay a fixed £1 and the other side covers the difference. Great for budgeting.
  • Option = the coupon. The right but not the obligation to buy at £1 — you only use it if it helps.
  • Asian / average-price = settle on the month's average. The price is the average over the whole month, not one day, so a single unlucky day can't sting you. (Oil cargoes work like this.)
  • Swing = a flexible season pass. Take a bit more or a bit less each week within agreed limits — handy when you don't know exactly how much you'll need.
  • Storage = having a cupboard. Buy chocolate cheap in summer, keep it, sell when it's dear in winter. The deal is really about the value of the cupboard.
  • Spread = bet on the gap, not the price. Not "is chocolate dear?" but "how big is the gap between chocolate and milk?" In power, electricity minus gas is the spark spread — a power station's margin.

And precisely:

  • Linear: futures, forwards, swaps.
  • Vanilla options on the above.
  • Exotics are common in commodities:
    • Asian / average-price options — payoff on the average of fixings over a period. Very common in oil & gas (a cargo prices off a monthly average, not a single day).
    • Swing options — the right to take more or less volume within contractual limits (classic gas supply optionality).
    • Storage optionality — value of injecting/withdrawing against the curve shape (calendar spread + intrinsic/extrinsic value).
    • Spread options — payoff on the difference between two underlyings: spark spread (power − gas, a generator's margin) and dark spread (power − coal).

Market data — the inputs

  • Forward curves — the spine of everything; one product can reference several curves.
  • Fixings / settlements — the daily marks that feed P&L (and that average-price products settle against).
  • Vol surfaces — by tenor and strike.
  • Correlations — essential for spread products and cross-commodity books.

This is exactly the "curves, fixings/settlements" language you'll see in the JD.

Why it's hard technically

Curves have many tenors; products reference multiple curves; and risk runs over many scenarios. Those multiply together, so a single book produces large volumes of results data — the engineering challenge is computing, storing, and aggregating per-bucket sensitivities and scenario P&L fast enough to be useful intraday.

Risk-language quick hits

  • P&L attribution / explain — decompose daily P&L into delta, gamma, vega, theta, new trades, and unexplained (the residual you have to justify).
  • VaR / scenario P&L — revalue the book under historical or hypothetical shocks to the curve and vol surface.
  • Intrinsic vs extrinsic value — especially for storage/swing: intrinsic = lock-in against today's curve; extrinsic = the optionality on top.
  • Basis — the spread between related points (location basis, e.g. one hub vs another; calendar basis between tenors).

Different kinds of trades

Beyond the instrument itself, "kinds of trade" usually means the intent and the structure.

By intent

  • Hedging — offset an existing exposure (a generator selling power forward; an airline buying fuel forward).
  • Proprietary / speculative — take a directional view to profit from price moves.
  • Arbitrage — lock a near-risk-free profit from a pricing inconsistency (location, calendar, exchange-vs-OTC).
  • Market-making — quote bid/offer continuously, earn the spread, manage the inventory risk it leaves.

By structure

  • Outright (directional) — a single long or short position in one contract.
  • Calendar spread — long one tenor, short another (buy winter / sell summer): a bet on curve shape, not level. Storage is essentially a calendar-spread play.
  • Location / basis spread — same commodity, two delivery points (TTF vs NBP; one power zone vs another).
  • Inter-commodity spread — two commodities: spark (power − gas), dark (power − coal), crack (refined product − crude).
  • Strip — buy/sell a run of consecutive contracts as one trade (a Cal-27 strip = every month of 2027).
  • Swap — exchange a floating index (e.g. monthly average) for a fixed price; the standard financial hedge.
  • Options — vanilla or exotic (Asian, swing) for non-linear / optional exposure.

Physical vs financial · venue

  • Physical — settles by actual delivery at a hub (you nominate volumes, deal with grid/pipeline).
  • Financial — cash-settled against an index/settlement price; no delivery.
  • Exchange-traded — standardised and centrally cleared (margin, no bilateral credit risk).
  • OTC / bilateral — bespoke; may be cleared or carry counterparty risk (ISDA/CSA).
  • Block / broker trade — large size done off the order book, then often given up to clearing.

Power & gas — markets, exchanges, clearing

Power

Electricity is (mostly) not storable, so supply and demand must balance in real time. That shapes the timeline:

  • Forwards / futures — months, quarters, seasons, calendar years ahead.
  • Day-ahead — an auction setting a price for each hour of tomorrow (the reference market).
  • Intraday — continuous trading up to close to real time, to fine-tune positions.
  • Balancing / imbalance — run by the grid operator (TSO) to keep the system stable; imbalance prices penalise being off-position.

Products quote as baseload (all hours), peak (e.g. 08:00–20:00) and off-peak, at hourly/half-hourly granularity. Exchanges: EPEX SPOT (day-ahead/intraday, Central-West Europe), Nord Pool (Nordics+), EEX (power futures), ICE. In the US, ISOs/RTOs run the markets: PJM, MISO, CAISO, ERCOT.

Gas

Gas is storable (storage fields, LNG), so the curve reflects seasonality and storage economics. Trading happens at virtual hubs in a pipeline network:

  • TTF (Netherlands) — the European benchmark.
  • NBP (UK).
  • Henry Hub (US benchmark, NYMEX).

Exchanges: ICE (TTF, NBP), EEX, CME/NYMEX (Henry Hub). LNG increasingly links regional gas markets toward one global market.

Clearing houses (CCPs)

A clearing house stands between buyer and seller — by novation it becomes the counterparty to both, so neither faces the other's credit. It nets exposures and collects margin:

  • Initial margin — collateral up front to cover potential future loss on default.
  • Variation margin — daily mark-to-market cash flows as prices move.
  • Default fund / waterfall — mutualised backstop if a member defaults.

Examples: ECC (European Commodity Clearing — clears EEX/EPEX), ICE Clear Europe, LCH, CME Clearing. Exchange trades are centrally cleared; OTC can be bilateral or cleared.

Basic concepts

  • Spot vs forward — near-term delivery vs an agreed future date.
  • Hub / delivery point — where the commodity is delivered and priced.
  • TSO / grid operator — runs the physical network and balancing.
  • Tenor & granularity — how far out, and how finely (hour / day / month / season / year).
  • Mark-to-market & margin — revalue daily; post collateral against the moves.
