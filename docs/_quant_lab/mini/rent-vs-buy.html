Should you rent or buy a London home? It's one of the most consequential financial decisions most adults make, and the honest answer depends on about twenty variables interacting in non-obvious ways. I built a calculator that does the math for you using real London data.

The inspiration was the New York Times rent vs buy calculator, originally launched in 2014 and updated in 2024. It's a brilliant tool — sliders, live feedback, and a "breakeven rent" headline that cuts through all the noise. But it's built for the US market, and applying its logic directly to London produces wrong answers for five or six structural reasons. This post walks through what I changed, what I kept, and what the calculator is honest about not knowing.

Why the NYT formula needed adapting

Five biggest differences between the US and UK markets:

  1. SDLT is tiered, not percentage. UK Stamp Duty Land Tax applies progressively: 0% up to £250k, 5% on £250k–£925k, 10% on £925k–£1.5M, 12% above. First-time buyers get a different schedule (0% up to £425k) but only below £625k total. The NYT uses a single "transfer tax percentage" field, which produces wildly wrong SDLT estimates on £600k+ purchases.
  2. No mortgage interest tax deduction. The UK abolished mortgage interest relief in 2000. The NYT calculator has an entire column for "tax savings on mortgage interest" — that column is zero in a UK calculator. Removing it actually makes buying look worse relative to the US.
  3. Council tax is a flat pound amount, not a percentage. NYT uses "property tax %" which is how US jurisdictions charge (1-2% of assessed value). UK council tax is set at a flat £ per year by borough and band — Wandsworth band D is ~£900 while Kingston band D is ~£2,320. These are valuations frozen in 1991, not tracked to current prices.
  4. UK mortgages are short-fix, not 30-year fixed. A typical UK mortgage has a 2- or 5-year fixed period, then reverts to a variable rate. When the fix ends you usually remortgage, paying £1-2k in fees. The NYT assumes one fixed rate for the whole term.
  5. Leasehold flats. Most London flats are leasehold, which means you own the flat for a fixed term (often 99-999 years from the original grant) and pay annual service charge + ground rent to the freeholder. As the lease runs down, the value declines — and below ~80 years, mortgage availability dries up and lease extension can cost tens of thousands. The NYT has no equivalent concept.

The core math

The fundamental idea is the same on both sides: sum up all the cash flowing out of your wallet over N years, then subtract what you get back.

Buying:

total_cost_of_buying = deposit + SDLT + legal + moving
                      + monthly_mortgage * 12 * N
                      + council_tax * N
                      + maintenance * N
                      + buildings_insurance * N
                      + (service_charge + ground_rent) * N   [flats only]
                      + remortgage_fees_per_fix_cycle
                      - home_value_at_sale
                      + remaining_mortgage
                      + selling_fee
                      - investment_return_on_excess_starting_cash

Renting:

total_cost_of_renting = rent_with_annual_growth_over_N_years
                       + moving_cost * num_moves
                       + renters_insurance * N
                       - investment_return_on_money_not_spent_on_deposit

The mortgage formula is the standard amortization equation, straight out of any finance textbook: 

def monthly_mortgage_payment(principal, annual_rate, years):
    if annual_rate == 0:
        return principal / (years * 12)
    r = annual_rate / 12
    n = years * 12
    return principal * r * (1 + r) ** n / ((1 + r) ** n - 1)

SDLT is slightly more fun — a bracket function with different thresholds for first-time buyers: 

STANDARD_BANDS = [
    (  250_000,      0.00),
    (  925_000,      0.05),
    (1_500_000,      0.10),
    (float("inf"),   0.12),
]

def calculate_sdlt(price, first_time_buyer=False):
    if first_time_buyer and price <= 625_000:
        return _tiered_tax(price, FTB_BANDS)
    return _tiered_tax(price, STANDARD_BANDS)

The breakeven rent is what makes the whole thing useful. If you know the total net cost of buying over N years, you can solve for the monthly rent at which the two sides tie: 

breakeven_monthly = (buy_net_cost + rent_investment_income
                    - non_rent_outflows) / (12 * growth_sum)

Where growth_sum is the discounted rent accumulator sum((1+g)^y for y in 0..N-1). If you can rent for less than this, renting wins. If not, buying wins.

LTV tiering — the detail that matters most in the UK

Here's a thing I didn't appreciate until I started building this: UK mortgage rates step down in brackets based on your loan-to-value ratio. At 95% LTV you might pay 6.0%; at 60% LTV you might pay 4.3%. That's a 1.5 percentage point gap, and over a 25-year £500k mortgage it's worth about £160,000 in interest.

This makes "save more before you buy" into one of the most impactful financial decisions a prospective UK buyer can make — and it's much sharper than the US equivalent.

The Bank of England publishes the typical quoted rates across lenders in Bankstats table G1.4 "Quoted household interest rates", by fix length (2/3/5/10 years) and LTV bracket (60/75/85/90/95%). The series codes are IUMBV* and you can fetch them as CSV from the BoE Interactive Statistical Database with no API key or login.

My calculator bundles a snapshot CSV and uses it to auto-suggest the mortgage rate based on the user's LTV. Drag the deposit slider left and the rate bumps up; drag it right and the rate drops. Watching the total cost of buying change in real time as you add another £10k to your deposit is the most concrete argument for saving more that I've seen in any calculator.

London-specific quirks

New build premium. New-build flats in London typically sell at a 10–20% premium to equivalent older properties. That premium often erodes over the first 5 years of ownership as the "new" stops being new. The calculator doesn't explicitly model this (it would need a depreciation curve) but warns about it in the UI.

Council tax bands. All UK residential property is in a council tax band (A–H) based on its 1991 valuation. Over 30 years of property appreciation, the bands have become increasingly meaningless. Wandsworth and Westminster have dramatically lower council tax than outer boroughs — Wandsworth band D is ~£900/year while Kingston band D is ~£2,320. The calculator uses the actual band D for the selected borough, not a percentage of current value.

Service charges on leasehold flats. Service charges have exploded in recent years — some new-build developments in London charge £5k-10k/year on top of everything else. The calculator includes service charge as a separate input visible only when the property type is "Flat".

Buildings vs contents insurance. If you buy a freehold house, you pay buildings insurance (~£300-500/year) because you own the bricks. If you buy a leasehold flat, the freeholder pays buildings insurance from the service charge (usually). Either way, both renters and buyers can pay optional contents insurance for their stuff.

First-time buyer SDLT relief. FTB relief bumps the nil-rate band from £250k to £425k, and the 5% band from £250k–£925k to £425k–£625k. Above £625k, FTB relief disappears entirely and you pay the full standard bands. For London FTBs this is the difference between ~£7,500 and £0 on a £450k flat.

Data sources

Everything the calculator uses is free and freely available under the UK Open Government Licence.

  • HM Land Registry Price Paid Data. 28 million property transactions since 1995, released as free downloadable CSVs. I filter to the most recent 3 years and group by (postcode district × property type × new build flag × bedrooms) to compute sensible median price defaults. Land Registry doesn't record bedroom counts itself — see the EPC join below for how I derive them.
  • EPC (Energy Performance Certificates). Every rental and sale in the UK requires an EPC, and the dataset is free to download per local authority via the EPC Open Data API. Each certificate records the number of habitable rooms, which I bucket into bedroom bands (Studio / 1 / 2 / 3 / 4+). A build script (scripts/build_ppd_with_bedrooms.py) downloads certificates for all 32 London local authorities, aggregates bedrooms by postcode, and joins them onto Land Registry. About 70% of recent London sales pick up a real bedroom band this way; the rest fall through to the legacy property-type filter.
  • ONS Private Rental Market Statistics. The Office for National Statistics publishes annual median rent by London borough under "Private Rental Market Summary Statistics in England", broken down further into Studio / 1-bed / 2-bed / 3-bed / 4+ bed bands (Table 2.7). I bundle two CSVs of the 33 London boroughs — one with the single median, one with the bedroom-segmented values — and update both annually.
  • Council tax. Each London borough publishes its annual council tax leaflet with band A-H rates. I aggregate them into a single CSV — manual curation, refreshed annually as boroughs set new rates each March.
  • Bank of England G1.4. Monthly snapshot of typical quoted household mortgage rates by fix length and LTV. I fetch it via the IADB interactive database as CSV. A helper script (scripts/refresh_boe_rates.py) pulls the latest values and writes the bundled snapshot. The Streamlit app reads the bundled file only — no network I/O at page load.

What this calculator gets right, what it doesn't

Right:

  • Real data defaults — price from HM Land Registry, rent from ONS, rate from BoE
  • Bedroom-aware defaults on both sides — a 1-bed flat in Camden defaults to a different price and rent than a 4+ bed terrace in the same borough
  • UK-specific costs: SDLT, council tax, service charge, ground rent
  • First-time buyer SDLT relief
  • LTV-tiered mortgage rates — the single biggest UK-specific factor
  • Opportunity cost of the deposit on both sides (buying and renting)
  • ISA protection toggle — the renter's investment return is less valuable if it's taxed
  • Multiple renter moves over a long horizon (offset by buyer remortgage fees)
  • All the formulas are open source and pytest-covered

Simplified or missing:

  • Deterministic single trajectory. House prices and investment returns are modeled as constant growth rates. Real markets have volatility. The 2008 crash was not a small event. A Monte Carlo version with confidence bands is on my list.
  • Borough-level rent. The ONS data is aggregated to borough × bedroom band, not postcode district. A 2-bed default for "Camden" lumps Somers Town and Hampstead together. The bedroom split helps but it's still a borough-wide median; postcode-district granularity would be the next step.
  • Fixed rate for the whole term. If your plan-to-stay is 15 years but you're on a 5-year fix, you'll remortgage twice at unknown future rates. The calculator keeps the rate fixed for the whole stay, with a caption warning.
  • No mortgage affordability stress test. Banks typically lend 4.5× income. If you earn £60k you can't actually get a £650k mortgage. The calculator doesn't check.
  • Interest-only mortgages not modeled. Some UK mortgages are interest-only (you pay interest during the term and owe the full principal at the end). Not common for owner-occupiers post-2014 but worth flagging.
  • Section 21 eviction risk. Until the Renters Reform Bill fully lands, UK tenants can be asked to leave with 2 months' notice for no reason. Not quantifiable but a real non-financial reason some people prefer buying.
  • Shared ownership. Buying 25–75% of a home and renting the rest — common in London, totally different math. Out of scope for this calculator but worth a dedicated tool.
  • LISA / Help to Buy bonuses. A Lifetime ISA gives a 25% government bonus on up to £4k/year of savings for first-time buyers. That effectively boosts your starting cash. The calculator's "starting cash" input is whatever number you type — it doesn't differentiate between cash saved in an ISA vs a normal savings account.
  • Emotional factors. Paint the walls. Stability for kids. Pride of ownership. Flexibility of renting. The NYT calculator acknowledges these in its footnotes. So do I: no calculator can tell you how much these are worth to you.

What I learned

Three things I hadn't fully internalized before I built this:

  1. LTV matters more than the base mortgage rate. A 10-point deposit shift (from 5% to 15% down) can save you more money than a 0.5 percentage point base rate change. It's counterintuitive until you realize the 10-point shift also moves you into a completely different rate tier.
  2. Council tax is invisibly huge. £2,000/year is £50,000 over a 25-year mortgage. That's the deposit for a parking space. Almost no one factors it in when thinking about "can I afford this flat".
  3. Nobody talks about the opportunity cost correctly. Every rent-vs-buy debate online eventually devolves into "but you're throwing money away on rent!" — which ignores that the alternative is throwing money into a house-shaped illiquid asset instead of a diversified investment portfolio. The honest comparison is total cost with opportunity cost on both sides, which is exactly what the NYT calculator gets right and what I tried to preserve.
