1. What Is a Yield Curve and Why It Matters

When a government borrows money, it issues bonds at different maturities — 1 year, 2 years, 5 years, 10 years, 30 years. Each maturity carries a different interest rate. Plot those rates against maturity and you get the yield curve.

The yield curve is the single most important object in fixed income. Every bond, swap, mortgage, and structured product is priced relative to it. It tells you the “risk-free” cost of borrowing at each horizon.

Three shapes you will see

  • Normal (upward-sloping): longer maturities pay higher rates. This is the usual state — investors demand more compensation for locking up money longer.
  • Flat: short and long rates are roughly equal. Often a transition between normal and inverted.
  • Inverted: short rates exceed long rates. Historically a reliable recession signal — the market expects the central bank to cut rates in the future.

Everything below builds on one idea: the yield curve comes in several “flavours” (par, spot, forward), and converting between them is the core skill of fixed income analysis.

2. Par Yields

The U.S. Treasury publishes par yields daily. A par yield for maturity n is the coupon rate at which a bond with that maturity would price at exactly $100 (par). In other words, if you build a bond that pays the par yield as its coupon and discount its cash flows at the “correct” rates, you get back $100.

Here is a sample par yield curve we will use throughout this post:

MaturityPar Yield
1Y4.50%
2Y4.70%
5Y4.90%
10Y5.00%
30Y5.10%

This is a normal, gently upward-sloping curve. A 30-year bond yields 60 basis points more than a 1-year bond.

Key point: par yields are not the discount rates you should use to price individual cash flows. For that you need spot rates.

3. Spot Rates (Zero-Coupon Rates) and Bootstrapping

What is a spot rate?

A spot rate sn is the yield on a zero-coupon bond maturing in n years. It is “pure” — one cash flow, one discount rate, no reinvestment ambiguity. The spot rate for year n answers: “What annual return does the market demand for a single payment received in n years?”

Why par ≠ spot

A par yield blends multiple spot rates together (one for each coupon date). A 5-year par bond has cash flows at years 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 — each discounted at a different spot rate. The par yield is a weighted average of those spot rates, weighted by present value of each cash flow. So the par curve and spot curve are related but not equal.

Bootstrapping: worked example

We extract spot rates from par yields one maturity at a time, starting from the shortest.

Step 1 — 1-Year spot rate

A 1-year par bond has only one cash flow at year 1: the coupon plus face value. There are no intermediate coupons, so the spot rate equals the par yield:

s1 = 4.50%

Step 2 — 2-Year spot rate

A 2-year bond with a 4.70% coupon (its par yield) prices at $100. Its cash flows are: $4.70 at year 1, and $104.70 at year 2. The pricing equation is:

100 = 4.70 / (1 + s1) + 104.70 / (1 + s2)2

Plug in s1 = 0.0450:

100 = 4.70 / 1.0450 + 104.70 / (1 + s2)2

100 = 4.4976 + 104.70 / (1 + s2)2

95.5024 = 104.70 / (1 + s2)2

(1 + s2)2 = 104.70 / 95.5024 = 1.09633

1 + s2 = 1.096330.5 = 1.04706

s2 = 4.706%

Notice this is slightly higher than the 2-year par yield of 4.70%. That is normal for an upward-sloping curve — spot rates sit above par yields for longer maturities because the par yield is “pulled down” by the earlier coupon discounted at a lower 1-year rate.

Step 3 — Handling gaps (years 3 and 4)

Our par curve jumps from 2Y to 5Y. To bootstrap the 5-year spot rate, we need spot rates for years 3 and 4. In practice we interpolate the par yields linearly:

  • 3Y par yield: 4.70% + (1/3)(4.90% − 4.70%) = 4.767%
  • 4Y par yield: 4.70% + (2/3)(4.90% − 4.70%) = 4.833%

Then we bootstrap s3 from the 3-year par bond, s4 from the 4-year par bond, and finally s5 from the 5-year par bond, each time using all previously computed spot rates.

Python: bootstrap the full spot curve

import math

# Par yields (annual coupon, annual compounding)
par = {1: 0.0450, 2: 0.0470, 3: 0.04767, 4: 0.04833, 5: 0.0490}
spot = {}

for n in sorted(par):
    c = par[n]                       # coupon rate = par yield
    # PV of all coupons before maturity, discounted at known spot rates
    pv_coupons = sum(c / (1 + spot[t]) ** t for t in range(1, n))
    # Solve: 100 = pv_coupons*100 + (1+c)*100 / (1+s_n)^n  =>  at par price=1
    # 1 = pv_coupons + (1 + c) / (1 + s_n)^n
    discount_factor = (1 - pv_coupons) / (1 + c)  # working in unit-face terms
    # discount_factor = 1 / (1 + s_n)^n
    s_n = (1 / discount_factor) ** (1 / n) - 1
    spot[n] = s_n
    print(f"s_{n} = {s_n:.4%}")

# Output:
# s_1 = 4.5000%
# s_2 = 4.7060%
# s_3 = 4.7783%
# s_4 = 4.8527%
# s_5 = 4.9213%

The spot curve lies slightly above the par curve at every maturity beyond 1 year — a hallmark of upward-sloping yield environments.

4. Forward Rates

What is a forward rate?

The forward rate fm,n is the interest rate implied by today’s spot curve for borrowing between future year m and future year n. It is not a forecast — it is a no-arbitrage rate locked in by today’s prices.

The no-arbitrage argument

Consider two strategies for investing $1 for 2 years:

  1. Buy a 2-year zero-coupon bond: you get (1 + s2)2 at year 2.
  2. Buy a 1-year zero, then reinvest at the 1-year forward: you get (1 + s1)(1 + f1,2) at year 2.

If these gave different amounts, you could arbitrage by going long one and short the other. So they must be equal:

(1 + s2)2 = (1 + s1)(1 + f1,2)

Worked example: the 1-year rate, 1 year from now

Using our bootstrapped spot rates, s1 = 4.50% and s2 = 4.706%:

(1.04706)2 = (1.0450)(1 + f1,2)

1.09633 = 1.0450 × (1 + f1,2)

1 + f1,2 = 1.09633 / 1.0450 = 1.04912

f1,2 = 4.912%

The 1-year forward rate one year from now (4.912%) is higher than either spot rate. This makes sense: to get a higher average return over 2 years than over 1 year, the second year must compensate with an even higher rate.

General formula for 1-year forward rates

fn−1,n = (1 + sn)n / (1 + sn−1)n−1 − 1 

# Forward rates from the spot curve
for n in range(2, 6):
    fwd = ((1 + spot[n]) ** n / (1 + spot[n - 1]) ** (n - 1)) - 1
    print(f"f_{n-1},{n} = {fwd:.4%}")

# Output:
# f_1,2 = 4.9124%
# f_2,3 = 4.9229%
# f_3,4 = 5.0762%
# f_4,5 = 5.1955%

The forward curve is steeper than the spot curve, which is steeper than the par curve. In an upward-sloping environment: par < spot < forward — always.

What forward rates tell you

Forward rates embed the market’s consensus (plus a term premium) about where short rates are headed. A rising forward curve says: the market prices in rate increases (or demands a premium for duration risk). An inverted forward curve says: the market expects rate cuts.

5. Nelson-Siegel Parametric Fitting

Why fit a smooth curve?

Market data is noisy — bid-ask bounces, liquidity differences, and on-the-run/off-the-run effects create scatter. A parametric model imposes economic structure with few parameters, giving you a smooth curve, economic interpretability, and the ability to interpolate and extrapolate.

The Nelson-Siegel formula

For maturity τ (in years), the Nelson-Siegel yield is:

y(τ) = β0 + β1 · [(1 − e−τ/λ) / (τ/λ)] + β2 · [(1 − e−τ/λ) / (τ/λ) − e−τ/λ]

What each parameter means

ParameterEconomic meaningEffect
β0Long-term levely(τ) → β0 as τ → ∞
β1Slope (short-term component)y(0) = β0 + β1; negative β1 = upward slope
β2Curvature (hump/trough)Controls the “belly” of the curve around medium maturities
λDecay speedWhere the hump peaks; larger λ pushes it out to longer maturities

Worked example

Given: β0 = 5.1, β1 = −0.5, β2 = −2.5, λ = 1.5. Compute y(1), y(5), and y(10).

First, define the helper terms for a given τ:

x = τ / λ,   A = (1 − e−x) / x,   B = A − e−x

τ = 1 year

x = 1 / 1.5 = 0.6667
e−0.6667 = 0.5134
A = (1 − 0.5134) / 0.6667 = 0.4866 / 0.6667 = 0.7299
B = 0.7299 − 0.5134 = 0.2165
y(1) = 5.1 + (−0.5)(0.7299) + (−2.5)(0.2165)
y(1) = 5.1 − 0.3650 − 0.5413 = 4.194%

τ = 5 years

x = 5 / 1.5 = 3.3333
e−3.3333 = 0.03567
A = (1 − 0.03567) / 3.3333 = 0.96433 / 3.3333 = 0.2893
B = 0.2893 − 0.03567 = 0.2536
y(5) = 5.1 + (−0.5)(0.2893) + (−2.5)(0.2536)
y(5) = 5.1 − 0.1447 − 0.6341 = 4.321%

τ = 10 years

x = 10 / 1.5 = 6.6667
e−6.6667 = 0.001272
A = (1 − 0.001272) / 6.6667 = 0.99873 / 6.6667 = 0.14981
B = 0.14981 − 0.001272 = 0.14854
y(10) = 5.1 + (−0.5)(0.14981) + (−2.5)(0.14854)
y(10) = 5.1 − 0.0749 − 0.3714 = 4.654%

The curve rises from 4.19% at 1 year to 4.65% at 10 years and approaches β0 = 5.10% at long maturities. The negative β2 creates a dip in the belly, pulling medium-term rates below where a simple slope would place them.

import math

def nelson_siegel(tau, b0, b1, b2, lam):
    """Nelson-Siegel yield for maturity tau (years)."""
    x = tau / lam
    exp_x = math.exp(-x)
    A = (1 - exp_x) / x
    B = A - exp_x
    return b0 + b1 * A + b2 * B

# Parameters
b0, b1, b2, lam = 5.1, -0.5, -2.5, 1.5

for tau in [0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 20, 30]:
    y = nelson_siegel(tau, b0, b1, b2, lam)
    print(f"  tau={tau:5.2f}  y = {y:.3f}%")

6. Svensson Extension

The Nelson-Siegel-Svensson (NSS) model adds a second curvature term with its own decay:

y(τ) = β0 + β1 · A1 + β2 · B1 + β3 · B2

where A1 and B1 use decay parameter λ1, and B2 = [(1 − e−τ/λ2) / (τ/λ2) − e−τ/λ2] uses a second decay λ2.

This gives the model a second hump (or trough), letting it fit curves with complex shapes at both short and long maturities. Six parameters instead of four.

When to use NSS vs NS

  • Nelson-Siegel (4 parameters): good enough for most government curves on most days. Parsimonious and stable.
  • Svensson (6 parameters): needed when the curve has a pronounced dip or second hump, common in Euro-area curves. The ECB publishes NSS parameters daily for Euro area sovereign curves.

In practice: start with NS. If residuals show a systematic pattern in the belly, upgrade to NSS.

7. Cubic Spline Interpolation

Instead of fitting a parametric model, you can run a cubic spline through your data points. A cubic spline is a piecewise polynomial (degree 3) that passes exactly through every observed point and is continuous in its first and second derivatives at the join points (knots).

Properties

  • Exact fit: zero residuals at every knot point. The curve reproduces every market rate exactly.
  • Smoothness: continuous first and second derivatives everywhere — no kinks.
  • Risk of oscillation: between knots, the cubic can overshoot or undershoot, especially if data points are unevenly spaced. This is called Runge’s phenomenon.

Spline vs parametric: when to use which

CriterionCubic splineNelson-Siegel / Svensson
GoalInterpolation (reproduce data exactly)Smoothing (impose economic structure)
Number of parametersGrows with data pointsFixed (4 or 6)
ExtrapolationUnreliable beyond last knotControlled (β0 anchors the long end)
Noise handlingPasses through noiseFilters noise
Use casePricing desk (need exact replication)Risk, research, cross-country comparison

Many central banks and dealers use both: a spline for day-to-day pricing and a parametric model for economic analysis and communication.

8. How They All Connect

Here is the full pipeline from raw market data to the objects used in pricing and risk:

  1. Observe par yields — Treasury auction results, dealer quotes.
  2. Bootstrap — extract the spot (zero-coupon) curve from par yields, one maturity at a time.
  3. Derive forward rates — compute the implied future rates from adjacent spot rates using the no-arbitrage formula.
  4. Fit a parametric model — run Nelson-Siegel or Svensson on the spot curve to get a smooth, interpretable description of the term structure.
  5. Or interpolate with splines — pass a cubic spline through the spot curve for exact repricing of observed instruments.

Downstream, these curves feed into:

  • Bond pricing: discount each cash flow at the appropriate spot rate.
  • OAS and Z-spread: find the spread over the spot curve that reprices a risky bond (see the OAS/Z-spread reference if available).
  • Swap valuation: the fixed leg of an interest rate swap is priced off the spot curve.
  • Credit analysis: the risk-free curve is the baseline; credit spreads measure the excess return demanded for default risk.

Master the chain par → spot → forward and you can navigate any fixed income market. Add a fitted curve on top and you have the toolkit that central banks, dealers, and quant desks use every day.
