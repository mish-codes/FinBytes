A Complete Walkthrough of Duration and Convexity

This post is a self-contained reference for the two most important measures of interest-rate risk: duration (first-order sensitivity) and convexity (second-order correction). Every concept comes with a fully worked numerical example so you can follow the arithmetic by hand, plus Python code you can run yourself.

1 — The Problem: Rates Move, Bond Prices Change

Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. When rates go up, the present value of a bond's future cash flows drops, so the price falls. When rates go down, the price rises.

But by how much? That is the key risk-management question. Two bonds with the same face value and maturity can react very differently to the same rate move. Duration answers "how much does the price change for a small rate move?" Convexity answers "is the duration estimate good enough for a large rate move?"

Together they give you the Taylor expansion of the price/yield relationship:

ΔP / P ≈ −Dmod × Δy  +  ½ × C × (Δy)²

where Dmod = modified duration, C = convexity, Δy = yield change

The first term (duration) is a straight line. The second term (convexity) captures the curvature. For small rate moves, duration alone is fine. For larger moves, you need both.

2 — Macaulay Duration

Definition: Macaulay duration is the weighted-average time to receive a bond's cash flows, where each cash flow is weighted by its present value as a fraction of the total price.

Dmac = ∑ [ t × PV(CFt) ] / Price

The result is in years. It tells you, on average, when you get your money back.

Worked example: 5% coupon, 3-year bond, yield = 6%

Face value = 100. Annual coupon = 5. Yield to maturity = 6%. Cash flows: $5 at years 1 and 2, $105 at year 3.

Step 1 — Present value of each cash flow:

Year 1:   5 / (1.06)^1 =   5 / 1.0600 =  4.7170
Year 2:   5 / (1.06)^2 =   5 / 1.1236 =  4.4500
Year 3: 105 / (1.06)^3 = 105 / 1.1910 = 88.1668

Price = 4.7170 + 4.4500 + 88.1668 = 97.333

Step 2 — Weight each PV by its time:

Year (t)CFPV(CF)t × PV(CF)
154.71701 × 4.7170 = 4.7170
254.45002 × 4.4500 = 8.9000
310588.16683 × 88.1668 = 264.5004
Sum:278.1174

Step 3 — Divide by price:

Dmac = 278.1174 / 97.333 = 2.857 years

Interpretation: on average, you receive your money back in 2.86 years. The 3-year maturity is pulled down because the coupon payments arrive earlier than the final principal.

3 — Modified Duration

Definition: Modified duration converts Macaulay duration into a direct measure of price sensitivity. It is the percentage price change per 1% change in yield.

Dmod = Dmac / (1 + y / k)

where y = yield to maturity and k = compounding frequency (k = 1 for annual, k = 2 for semiannual).

Worked example (continuing from above)

Annual compounding (k = 1), y = 6%:

Dmod = 2.857 / (1 + 0.06 / 1) = 2.857 / 1.06 = 2.695

Meaning: if the yield rises by 1% (100 bps), the bond price falls by approximately 2.695%.

Verification: reprice at 7%

Year 1:   5 / (1.07)^1 =   5 / 1.0700 =  4.6729
Year 2:   5 / (1.07)^2 =   5 / 1.1449 =  4.3672
Year 3: 105 / (1.07)^3 = 105 / 1.2250 = 85.7117

Price at 7% = 4.6729 + 4.3672 + 85.7117 = 94.752

Actual percentage change:

ΔP / P = (94.752 − 97.333) / 97.333 = −2.581 / 97.333 = −2.652%

Duration estimate: −2.695%. Actual: −2.652%. The difference of 0.043 percentage points comes from the curvature of the price/yield relationship — that is exactly what convexity captures.

4 — Dollar Duration (DV01 / BPV)

Definition: DV01 (dollar value of one basis point) is the dollar change in price for a 1 bp (0.01%) move in yield. Also called BPV (basis point value).

DV01 = Dmod × Price × 0.0001

DV01 is quoted per 100 face value. To scale to a portfolio position:

Portfolio DV01 = DV01 × (Notional / 100)

Worked example: $10 million position

Bond: Dmod = 5.2, clean price = 98.50 per 100 face, position = $10,000,000 notional.

DV01 per 100 face = 5.2 × 98.50 × 0.0001 = $0.05122

Portfolio DV01 = 0.05122 × (10,000,000 / 100) = $5,122 per basis point

If rates rise by 10 bps:

P&L = −$5,122 × 10 = −$51,220

A 10 bp rate rise costs this portfolio $51,220. DV01 is the number traders and risk managers use daily to size hedges and set risk limits.

5 — Key Duration Relationships

Several intuitive rules govern how duration behaves:

  • Zero-coupon bond: Dmac = maturity. All cash flow arrives at the end, so the weighted-average time equals the maturity itself.
  • Higher coupon → lower duration. More cash flow arrives early, pulling the weighted average toward the present.
  • Longer maturity → higher duration. Cash flows extend further into the future.
  • Higher yield → slightly lower duration. Higher discount rates shrink the PV of distant cash flows more than near ones, pulling the average closer.

Python: compute Macaulay and modified duration

def duration(coupon_rate, face, maturity, ytm, freq=1):
    """Compute Macaulay and modified duration for a fixed-rate bond.

    Args:
        coupon_rate: annual coupon rate (e.g., 0.05 for 5%)
        face: face value (e.g., 100)
        maturity: years to maturity
        ytm: yield to maturity (e.g., 0.06 for 6%)
        freq: compounding frequency (1 = annual, 2 = semiannual)

    Returns:
        (price, mac_duration, mod_duration)
    """
    n_periods = int(maturity * freq)
    coupon = coupon_rate * face / freq
    y = ytm / freq

    price = 0.0
    weighted_time = 0.0

    for t in range(1, n_periods + 1):
        cf = coupon if t < n_periods else coupon + face
        pv = cf / (1 + y) ** t
        price += pv
        weighted_time += (t / freq) * pv    # t/freq converts periods to years

    mac_dur = weighted_time / price
    mod_dur = mac_dur / (1 + y)

    return price, mac_dur, mod_dur


# Example: 5% coupon, 3-year, 6% yield
price, mac, mod = duration(0.05, 100, 3, 0.06)
print(f"Price:     {price:.3f}")
print(f"Mac Dur:   {mac:.3f} years")
print(f"Mod Dur:   {mod:.3f}")
# Output:
#   Price:     97.333
#   Mac Dur:   2.857 years
#   Mod Dur:   2.695

6 — Convexity

Definition: Convexity is the second derivative of the price/yield relationship, scaled by the price. It measures how much duration itself changes as yields move. A straight-line (zero convexity) approximation undershoots when rates fall and overshoots when rates rise. Convexity corrects this.

Convexity = ∑ [ t(t+1) × PV(CFt) ] / [ Price × (1 + y)² ]

Worked example: same 3-year bond (5% coupon, 6% yield)

We already have the PV of each cash flow from Section 2. Now compute t(t+1) × PV for each:

Year (t)PV(CF)t(t+1)t(t+1) × PV
14.71701 × 2 = 22 × 4.7170 = 9.4340
24.45002 × 3 = 66 × 4.4500 = 26.7000
388.16683 × 4 = 1212 × 88.1668 = 1058.0016
Sum:1094.1356

Divide by Price × (1 + y)²:

Denominator = 97.333 × (1.06)² = 97.333 × 1.1236 = 109.365

Convexity = 1094.1356 / 109.365 = 10.005

The full approximation formula

ΔP / P ≈ −Dmod × Δy  +  ½ × Convexity × (Δy)²

Verification: large rate move (yield 6% → 8%, Δy = +2%)

Duration-only estimate:

ΔP / P ≈ −2.695 × 0.02 = −0.05390 = −5.390%

Duration + convexity estimate:

ΔP / P ≈ −0.05390 + 0.5 × 10.005 × (0.02)²
       = −0.05390 + 0.5 × 10.005 × 0.0004
       = −0.05390 + 0.00200
       = −0.05190 = −5.190%

Actual (reprice at 8%):

Year 1:   5 / (1.08)^1 =   5 / 1.0800 =  4.6296
Year 2:   5 / (1.08)^2 =   5 / 1.1664 =  4.2867
Year 3: 105 / (1.08)^3 = 105 / 1.2597 = 83.3531

Price at 8% = 4.6296 + 4.2867 + 83.3531 = 92.269

Actual ΔP / P = (92.269 − 97.333) / 97.333 = −5.203%

Comparison:

MethodEstimated ΔP/PError vs Actual
Duration only−5.390%0.187% too negative
Duration + convexity−5.190%0.013% too positive
Actual (repricing)−5.203%—

The convexity correction reduced the error from 0.187% to 0.013% — an improvement of over 14x. For a $100 million portfolio, that is the difference between being off by $187,000 versus $13,000.

Python: compute convexity and full approximation

def convexity(coupon_rate, face, maturity, ytm, freq=1):
    """Compute convexity for a fixed-rate bond."""
    n_periods = int(maturity * freq)
    coupon = coupon_rate * face / freq
    y = ytm / freq

    price = 0.0
    conv_sum = 0.0

    for t in range(1, n_periods + 1):
        cf = coupon if t < n_periods else coupon + face
        pv = cf / (1 + y) ** t
        price += pv
        conv_sum += t * (t + 1) * pv

    cx = conv_sum / (price * (1 + y) ** 2)
    return cx


def price_change_approx(mod_dur, cx, delta_y):
    """Estimate percentage price change using duration + convexity."""
    return -mod_dur * delta_y + 0.5 * cx * delta_y ** 2


# Example: 5% coupon, 3-year, yield moves from 6% to 8%
_, mac, mod = duration(0.05, 100, 3, 0.06)
cx = convexity(0.05, 100, 3, 0.06)
approx = price_change_approx(mod, cx, 0.02)

print(f"Mod Duration: {mod:.3f}")
print(f"Convexity:    {cx:.3f}")
print(f"Approx dP/P:  {approx:.5f}  ({approx * 100:.3f}%)")
# Output:
#   Mod Duration: 2.695
#   Convexity:    10.005
#   Approx dP/P:  -0.05190  (-5.190%)

7 — Why Convexity Is Good for Bondholders

For an option-free bond, convexity is always positive. This means the price/yield curve bows outward (it is concave upward when viewed from the yield axis). The practical consequence:

  • When rates fall: the price rises more than duration alone predicts.
  • When rates rise: the price falls less than duration alone predicts.

In both directions, convexity works in the bondholder's favor. You win more than you lose. This is why, all else equal, investors prefer bonds with higher convexity — and they accept a slightly lower yield for it.

Numerical demonstration

Using our 3-year bond (Dmod = 2.695, convexity = 10.005, price = 97.333):

Yield moveDuration-only ΔP/PActual ΔP/PConvexity benefit
−2% (6% → 4%) +5.390% +5.604% +0.214% extra gain
+2% (6% → 8%) −5.390% −5.203% 0.187% less loss

Rates down 2%: you gain 5.60% instead of 5.39%. Rates up 2%: you lose only 5.20% instead of 5.39%. The asymmetry is convexity at work.

Callable bonds: negative convexity

A callable bond behaves differently. When rates fall far enough, the issuer will call the bond at the call price (typically par). This caps the upside — the price cannot rise much above the call price. The result is negative convexity in the low-rate region:

Option-free bond:  rates fall → price rises freely         (positive convexity)
Callable bond:     rates fall → price rises, then flattens  (negative convexity)

At very low rates, the callable bond's price is "compressed" toward the call price.
Duration actually decreases as rates fall (it shortens toward the call date).

This is why callable bonds offer a higher yield — the bondholder is compensated for giving up convexity. The OAS framework (covered in the bond pricing post) strips out this option effect to isolate the pure credit spread.

8 — Duration vs Spread Duration (CS01)

A corporate bond is exposed to two independent sources of rate risk:

  1. Interest-rate duration: sensitivity to changes in the risk-free rate (Treasury yields).
  2. Spread duration (CS01): sensitivity to changes in the credit spread.

They can — and do — move independently:

ScenarioRatesSpreadsBond price
Risk-off flight to quality↓↑Depends on which dominates
Economic recovery↑↓Depends on which dominates
Central bank hike + recession fear↑↑Falls on both fronts
Rate cuts + credit optimism↓↓Rises on both fronts

The total P&L approximation for a single bond position is:

ΔP ≈ −DV01 × Δrate(bps) − CS01 × Δspread(bps) × (Notional / 100)

Worked example

A $5M corporate bond position: Dmod = 4.8, price = 99.00, CS01 = 0.048 per 100 face.

Rate DV01 = 4.8 × 99.00 × 0.0001 = $0.04752 per 100 face
Portfolio rate DV01 = 0.04752 × 50,000 = $2,376 per bp

Portfolio CS01 = 0.048 × 50,000 = $2,400 per bp of spread

Scenario: rates rise 15 bps, spreads widen 25 bps.

Rate P&L   = −$2,376 × 15 = −$35,640
Spread P&L = −$2,400 × 25 = −$60,000
Total P&L  =                   −$95,640

Spread risk ($60,000) dominates rate risk ($35,640) in this scenario. Portfolio managers track both metrics separately and hedge each with different instruments — interest-rate swaps for duration, CDS for spread risk.

9 — How It Connects to QuantLab

Duration and convexity are foundational inputs to the risk frameworks built in the QuantLab exercise series:

  • Interest-rate VaR: Duration and convexity feed into the parametric VaR calculation. The portfolio DV01 tells you how much P&L volatility to expect from rate moves. Combined with historical rate-change volatility, you get interest-rate VaR.
  • Credit VaR (Exercise 20): CS01 (spread duration) is the parallel concept for credit spreads. The Monte Carlo credit VaR engine simulates correlated spread changes and uses CS01 to convert them into portfolio P&L.
  • Binomial trees (Exercise 19): When duration approximation is not accurate enough — for example, with callable or putable bonds — you price directly on a rate tree using backward induction. The tree naturally captures both duration and convexity effects without needing to compute them separately.
  • OAS vs Z-spread: The difference between OAS and Z-spread for a callable bond is driven by negative convexity in the low-rate region. The binomial tree prices the option; the OAS strips it out. Duration and convexity explain why the two spreads differ.

The full chain for interest-rate risk:

Cash flows → Duration + Convexity → DV01 → Rate VaR

The full chain for credit risk:

Cash flows → Spread Duration (CS01) → Credit VaR (Monte Carlo)

Both feed into total portfolio risk, which accounts for correlations
between rate moves and spread moves across all positions.

Python: full bond risk dashboard

def bond_risk_report(coupon_rate, face, maturity, ytm, notional, freq=1):
    """Print a complete duration/convexity/DV01 risk report."""
    price, mac_dur, mod_dur = duration(coupon_rate, face, maturity, ytm, freq)
    cx = convexity(coupon_rate, face, maturity, ytm, freq)
    dv01_per_100 = mod_dur * price * 0.0001
    portfolio_dv01 = dv01_per_100 * (notional / face)

    print(f"{'Bond Risk Report':=^50}")
    print(f"Coupon:         {coupon_rate * 100:.2f}%")
    print(f"Maturity:       {maturity} years")
    print(f"YTM:            {ytm * 100:.2f}%")
    print(f"Price:          {price:.3f} per 100 face")
    print(f"Notional:       ${notional:,.0f}")
    print(f"{'':=^50}")
    print(f"Macaulay Dur:   {mac_dur:.3f} years")
    print(f"Modified Dur:   {mod_dur:.3f}")
    print(f"Convexity:      {cx:.3f}")
    print(f"DV01 (per 100): ${dv01_per_100:.5f}")
    print(f"Portfolio DV01: ${portfolio_dv01:,.2f} per bp")
    print(f"{'':=^50}")

    # Scenario analysis
    for shock_bps in [-50, -25, -10, 10, 25, 50, 100]:
        dy = shock_bps / 10000
        approx_pct = -mod_dur * dy + 0.5 * cx * dy ** 2
        approx_pnl = approx_pct * price * (notional / face)
        print(f"  {shock_bps:+4d} bps: dP/P = {approx_pct * 100:+.3f}%"
              f"  P&L = ${approx_pnl:+,.0f}")
