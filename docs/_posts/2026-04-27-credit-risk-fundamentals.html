This post is a self-contained reference on credit risk quantification — the chain that starts with an observable bond spread and ends with a dollar-denominated expected loss. Every formula gets a worked numerical example you can verify by hand or in Python.

1 — Credit Spreads

Definition

A credit spread is the difference between the yield on a corporate bond and the yield on a risk-free government bond of the same maturity:

credit spread = y_corporate − y_risk-free

Measured in basis points (bps). 1% = 100 bps.

The spread compensates investors for three things:

  • Default risk — the chance the issuer fails to pay.
  • Liquidity premium — corporate bonds trade less frequently than Treasuries, so investors demand extra yield for the inconvenience of selling.
  • Supply and demand — new issuance, index rebalancing, and risk-appetite shifts all move spreads independently of fundamentals.

Spread hierarchy by rating

Typical ranges in normal markets:

  • AAA: 50–80 bps. Minimal credit risk priced in.
  • BBB: 120–200 bps. Lowest investment-grade bucket; sensitive to the business cycle.
  • BB: 300–500 bps. Top of high yield; jumps sharply in downturns.
  • B and below: 500+ bps. Distressed territory in a crisis can exceed 1000 bps.

These ranges are observable in the ICE BofA index family on FRED: BAMLC0A1CAAA (AAA), BAMLC0A4CBBB (BBB), BAMLH0A1HYBB (BB), BAMLH0A2HYB (B). Daily updates, free data, excellent for backtesting.

Worked example

Ford 5Y bond yield:    5.50%
5Y Treasury yield:     4.00%
Credit spread:         5.50% − 4.00% = 1.50% = 150 bps

Interpretation: investors demand 150 basis points of extra annual yield to hold Ford debt instead of a Treasury. If Treasuries stay flat but the Ford spread widens to 200 bps, the bond price falls — the market is pricing in higher default risk (or lower liquidity, or both). Spread tightening is the reverse: falling spreads mean improving credit conditions or rising risk appetite.

2 — Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

What it is

A CDS is an insurance contract on a bond. Two counterparties:

  • Protection buyer: pays a periodic premium (the "CDS spread") to the seller.
  • Protection seller: if the reference entity defaults, pays the buyer the loss amount (par minus recovery value).

No bond changes hands (it's a derivative). The CDS spread is therefore a cleaner measure of default risk than the bond spread — it strips out coupon effects, callability, and most liquidity noise.

CDS Big Bang (2009)

Before 2009, every CDS had a bespoke coupon equal to the market spread at trade time. After the ISDA "Big Bang" standardisation:

  • Fixed coupons: 100 bps (investment grade) or 500 bps (high yield).
  • Any difference between the market spread and the fixed coupon is settled as an upfront payment at trade inception.
  • Standardised quarterly payment dates (Mar 20, Jun 20, Sep 20, Dec 20).

This made CDS fungible and clearable — essential for the post-crisis push toward central clearing.

Worked example

5-year CDS on Company X, notional $10,000,000, CDS spread = 150 bps, recovery assumption = 40%.

Annual premium   = $10,000,000 × (150 / 10,000)
                 = $10,000,000 × 0.0150
                 = $150,000 per year

Quarterly premium = $150,000 / 4 = $37,500

If default occurs:
  Seller pays = Notional × (1 − Recovery)
              = $10,000,000 × (1 − 0.40)
              = $10,000,000 × 0.60
              = $6,000,000

The protection buyer pays $37,500 every quarter for up to 5 years ($750,000 total if no default). In exchange, they are insured against a $6,000,000 loss. Whether that trade is "worth it" depends on the actual probability of default — which is what the hazard-rate model extracts.

3 — Recovery Rates and Loss Given Default

What recovery means

Recovery rate (R) is the fraction of par value bondholders get back after a default — through asset liquidation, restructuring, or legal settlement. Loss Given Default (LGD) is the complement:

LGD = 1 − R

Standard assumptions by seniority

SeniorityTypical RecoveryLGD
Senior secured~60%40%
Senior unsecured~40%60%
Subordinated~20%80%

Why 40% is the magic number

ISDA's standard CDS contract assumes 40% recovery for senior unsecured debt. This is the default input for CDS pricing worldwide. It's not arbitrary — Moody's long-run average recovery for senior unsecured bonds is roughly 37–42%, depending on the measurement period and the weighting method (issuer-weighted vs. volume-weighted). The convention simplifies the market: everyone prices off the same R, so the CDS spread is a direct apples-to-apples comparison across issuers.

If you believe actual recovery will be higher than 40%, the CDS is cheap (you're paying a premium based on a loss that's overstated). If you think recovery will be lower, the CDS is expensive. Recovery-rate views are a distinct axis of credit trading.

4 — Hazard Rate Model

Setup

The hazard rate λ (lambda) is the instantaneous probability of default at time t, conditional on having survived to t. Under the constant-hazard assumption, default follows an exponential process — the same math as radioactive decay.

Key formulas

Survival probability:   S(T) = exp(−λT)
Default probability:    PD(T) = 1 − exp(−λT)
CDS spread relation:    s ≈ λ × (1 − R)
Inversion:              λ ≈ s / (1 − R)

Intuition for the CDS relation: per unit time, the protection seller expects to pay out λ × (1 − R) — the probability of default times the loss given default. The premium the buyer pays must equal this expected payout for the trade to be fair. Observe the premium, invert for λ.

Full worked example

Given: CDS spread s = 150 bps, recovery R = 40%.

Step 1: Convert spread to decimal
  s = 150 / 10,000 = 0.0150

Step 2: Compute LGD
  LGD = 1 − R = 1 − 0.40 = 0.60

Step 3: Solve for hazard rate
  λ = s / LGD = 0.0150 / 0.60 = 0.025  (2.5% annual hazard rate)

Step 4: Survival probabilities
  S(1) = exp(−0.025 × 1) = exp(−0.025) = 0.9753  → 97.53%
  S(2) = exp(−0.025 × 2) = exp(−0.050) = 0.9512  → 95.12%
  S(3) = exp(−0.025 × 3) = exp(−0.075) = 0.9277  → 92.77%
  S(5) = exp(−0.025 × 5) = exp(−0.125) = 0.8825  → 88.25%

Step 5: Default probabilities
  PD(1) = 1 − S(1) = 1 − 0.9753 = 0.0247  → 2.47%
  PD(2) = 1 − S(2) = 1 − 0.9512 = 0.0488  → 4.88%
  PD(3) = 1 − S(3) = 1 − 0.9277 = 0.0723  → 7.23%
  PD(5) = 1 − S(5) = 1 − 0.8825 = 0.1175  → 11.75%

Key observation: a 2.5% annual hazard doesn't sound alarming, but over 5 years it compounds to an 11.75% cumulative default probability. Time is the multiplier.

Python implementation

import math

def hazard_from_cds(spread_bps: float, recovery: float = 0.40) -> float:
    """Implied constant hazard rate from CDS spread."""
    return (spread_bps / 10_000) / (1 - recovery)

def survival_prob(lam: float, t: float) -> float:
    """Survival probability under constant hazard."""
    return math.exp(-lam * t)

def default_prob(lam: float, t: float) -> float:
    """Cumulative default probability under constant hazard."""
    return 1 - math.exp(-lam * t)

# Example: 150 bps CDS, 40% recovery
lam = hazard_from_cds(150)          # 0.025
for t in [1, 2, 3, 5]:
    s = survival_prob(lam, t)
    d = default_prob(lam, t)
    print(f"T={t}Y  S={s:.4f}  PD={d:.4f}")

# Output:
# T=1Y  S=0.9753  PD=0.0247
# T=2Y  S=0.9512  PD=0.0488
# T=3Y  S=0.9277  PD=0.0723
# T=5Y  S=0.8825  PD=0.1175

5 — Piecewise Hazard Rates

Why constant λ is too simple

CDS contracts trade at multiple tenors (1Y, 3Y, 5Y, 7Y, 10Y), and the spreads are generally not flat across tenors. A 1Y CDS at 80 bps and a 5Y CDS at 150 bps imply that the market sees default risk increasing over time. A single constant λ can't capture that shape.

The fix: piecewise-constant hazard rates. Assume λ is constant within each interval [Ti-1, Ti] but can jump between intervals. Bootstrap from the short end outward, just like you would with a yield curve.

Worked example

CDS term structure: {1Y: 80 bps, 3Y: 120 bps, 5Y: 150 bps}. Recovery R = 40%, so LGD = 0.60.

Step 1: Average hazard rate implied by each tenor
  λ_avg(0–1Y) = (80 / 10,000) / 0.60  = 0.008 / 0.60 = 0.01333
  λ_avg(0–3Y) = (120 / 10,000) / 0.60 = 0.012 / 0.60 = 0.02000
  λ_avg(0–5Y) = (150 / 10,000) / 0.60 = 0.015 / 0.60 = 0.02500

Step 2: Cumulative hazard at each tenor
  Λ(1) = λ_avg(0–1) × 1 = 0.01333
  Λ(3) = λ_avg(0–3) × 3 = 0.06000
  Λ(5) = λ_avg(0–5) × 5 = 0.12500

Step 3: Strip out interval hazard rates (forward hazards)
  λ(0–1Y) = 0.01333  (direct from Step 1)
  λ(1–3Y) = (Λ(3) − Λ(1)) / (3 − 1)
           = (0.06000 − 0.01333) / 2
           = 0.04667 / 2
           = 0.02333
  λ(3–5Y) = (Λ(5) − Λ(3)) / (5 − 3)
           = (0.12500 − 0.06000) / 2
           = 0.06500 / 2
           = 0.03250

Step 4: Interpretation
  0–1Y:  λ = 1.33%   (low near-term risk)
  1–3Y:  λ = 2.33%   (rising)
  3–5Y:  λ = 3.25%   (highest)
  Risk is back-loaded: the market prices more danger in years 3–5.

Python implementation

import math

def bootstrap_piecewise_hazards(
    tenors: list[float],
    spreads_bps: list[float],
    recovery: float = 0.40,
) -> list[dict]:
    """Bootstrap piecewise-constant hazard rates from a CDS curve."""
    lgd = 1 - recovery
    results = []
    prev_cum_hazard = 0.0
    prev_tenor = 0.0

    for tenor, spread in zip(tenors, spreads_bps):
        avg_hazard = (spread / 10_000) / lgd
        cum_hazard = avg_hazard * tenor
        interval_hazard = (cum_hazard - prev_cum_hazard) / (tenor - prev_tenor)
        results.append({
            "interval": f"{prev_tenor:.0f}-{tenor:.0f}Y",
            "lambda": round(interval_hazard, 5),
            "cum_hazard": round(cum_hazard, 5),
            "survival": round(math.exp(-cum_hazard), 4),
        })
        prev_cum_hazard = cum_hazard
        prev_tenor = tenor

    return results

# Example
curve = bootstrap_piecewise_hazards([1, 3, 5], [80, 120, 150])
for row in curve:
    print(row)

# Output:
# {'interval': '0-1Y', 'lambda': 0.01333, 'cum_hazard': 0.01333, 'survival': 0.9868}
# {'interval': '1-3Y', 'lambda': 0.02333, 'cum_hazard': 0.06,    'survival': 0.9418}
# {'interval': '3-5Y', 'lambda': 0.0325,  'cum_hazard': 0.125,   'survival': 0.8825}

6 — Survival Curves

What a survival curve shows

S(T) plotted against T starts at 1.0 (the entity is alive today) and decreases monotonically. The shape encodes the risk profile:

  • Convex curve (drops steeply early, flattens later): front-loaded risk. The market thinks danger is near-term — typical for distressed issuers.
  • Concave curve (drops slowly early, steepens later): back-loaded risk. Near-term looks safe, but long-term uncertainty is high — typical for a BBB company in a late-cycle economy.
  • Linear curve: roughly constant hazard rate. Risk is evenly distributed over time.

Worked example: survival values from the piecewise model

Using the hazard rates from Section 5:

T = 0:  S(0) = 1.0000   (alive today, by definition)
T = 1:  S(1) = exp(−0.01333)           = 0.9868
T = 2:  S(2) = exp(−0.01333 − 0.02333) = exp(−0.03667) = 0.9640
T = 3:  S(3) = exp(−0.06000)             = 0.9418
T = 4:  S(4) = exp(−0.06000 − 0.03250) = exp(−0.09250) = 0.9117
T = 5:  S(5) = exp(−0.12500)             = 0.8825

Verification: S(2) uses the interval 1–3Y hazard rate (λ = 0.02333) for one year beyond T=1. Cumulative hazard at T=2 is 0.01333 + 0.02333 × 1 = 0.03667.

This curve is concave — the drop from T=3 to T=5 (0.9418 → 0.8825, a 5.93 pp decline over 2 years) is steeper than the drop from T=0 to T=2 (1.0000 → 0.9640, a 3.60 pp decline over 2 years). Consistent with the back-loaded risk we identified in the piecewise hazards.

7 — Expected Loss

Formula

Expected Loss = PD × LGD × EAD

Where:

  • PD: probability of default over the horizon.
  • LGD: loss given default (1 − recovery rate).
  • EAD: exposure at default — how much is at risk when default happens.

Worked example

A bank holds $1,000,000 in senior unsecured bonds of Company X. The 5Y CDS spread is 150 bps. Recovery assumption: 40%.

Step 1: Hazard rate (from Section 4)
  λ = 0.025

Step 2: 5-year default probability
  PD(5) = 1 − exp(−0.025 × 5) = 1 − 0.8825 = 0.1175  (11.75%)

Step 3: Loss given default
  LGD = 1 − 0.40 = 0.60  (60%)

Step 4: Exposure at default
  EAD = $1,000,000

Step 5: Expected loss
  EL = PD × LGD × EAD
     = 0.1175 × 0.60 × $1,000,000
     = $70,500

The bank should expect to lose $70,500 on this position over 5 years, on average. This number drives several things:

  • Loan loss reserves: the bank must set aside capital to absorb expected losses (IFRS 9 / CECL accounting standards).
  • Credit limits: the expected loss per counterparty feeds into concentration risk thresholds.
  • Pricing: the loan's interest rate must cover EL plus a return on the regulatory capital tied up by the exposure.

Basel connection

Under Basel II/III, the same three inputs — PD, LGD, EAD — determine regulatory capital. Banks using the Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach estimate PD from their own models, and the Basel formula computes a capital requirement that covers both expected and unexpected losses at the 99.9th percentile. The chain is identical: spread → hazard rate → PD → capital.

Python implementation

import math

def expected_loss(
    spread_bps: float,
    recovery: float,
    horizon_years: float,
    exposure: float,
) -> dict:
    """Compute expected loss from CDS spread, recovery, horizon, and exposure."""
    lam = (spread_bps / 10_000) / (1 - recovery)
    pd = 1 - math.exp(-lam * horizon_years)
    lgd = 1 - recovery
    el = pd * lgd * exposure
    return {
        "hazard_rate": round(lam, 5),
        "pd": round(pd, 4),
        "lgd": round(lgd, 2),
        "ead": exposure,
        "expected_loss": round(el, 2),
    }

result = expected_loss(spread_bps=150, recovery=0.40, horizon_years=5, exposure=1_000_000)
print(result)

# Output:
# {'hazard_rate': 0.025, 'pd': 0.1175, 'lgd': 0.6, 'ead': 1000000, 'expected_loss': 70500.0}

8 — How It All Connects

The entire credit risk quantification chain:

Bond yield − Treasury yield
         ↓
   Credit spread (bps)
         ↓
  ≈ CDS spread (cleaner, no liquidity/coupon noise)
         ↓
   λ = spread / (1 − R)        [hazard rate]
         ↓
   S(T) = exp(−λT)              [survival curve]
   PD(T) = 1 − S(T)               [default probability]
         ↓
   EL = PD × LGD × EAD          [expected loss in dollars]
         ↓
   Regulatory capital, loan pricing, portfolio risk

Each step transforms one observable (the yield, the spread) into something more useful downstream. The math is not complicated — it's exponentials and multiplication — but the chain matters because it connects what bond traders see on a screen to what a bank's risk department reports to regulators.

Caveats and where to go deeper

  • Constant vs. piecewise λ: the constant-hazard model is a useful approximation. For real pricing, you bootstrap a piecewise hazard curve from the full CDS term structure (Section 5).
  • The s ≈ λ(1−R) shortcut: this ignores discounting, accrual-on-default, and the timing of premium payments. A full CDS pricer matches the present value of the premium leg to the protection leg under the risk-neutral measure.
  • Recovery is not fixed: 40% is a convention, not a law. Actual recoveries vary by issuer, seniority, economic conditions, and jurisdiction.
  • CDS ≠ bond spread: the CDS-bond basis (CDS spread minus bond spread) can be positive or negative, driven by funding costs, counterparty risk, and supply/demand in each market.
  • Correlation matters for portfolios: expected loss for a single name is straightforward. For a portfolio, you need a model of default correlation — that's where copulas and Monte Carlo simulation enter (see the portfolio credit VaR exercise in QuantLab).

The formulas in this post are the foundation. Everything in credit risk — from pricing a single CDS to running a portfolio VaR simulation — builds on this chain.
