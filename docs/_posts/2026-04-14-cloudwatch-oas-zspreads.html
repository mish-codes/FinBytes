Why Two Spreads?

If you price a corporate bond by discounting its cash flows off the spot curve, the spread you need to add to match the market price is the Z-spread (zero-volatility spread). It's a single number, easy to compute, and fine for bullet bonds with no embedded options.

But many corporate bonds are callable — the issuer can redeem them early, typically at par, after a lockout period. That's an option the issuer holds, and it has value. When rates drop, the issuer calls the bond and refinances cheaper. The bondholder loses upside. The Z-spread lumps the credit spread and the option cost together. The OAS (Option-Adjusted Spread) strips the option out, giving you a purer measure of credit compensation.

For non-callable bonds: OAS ≈ Z-spread. For callable bonds: OAS < Z-spread, because some of the Z-spread was compensating for the call option, not for credit risk.

The Binomial Rate Tree

To value the embedded option, you need a model that generates different interest rate scenarios. The simplest is a recombining binomial tree:

At each step, the rate either:
  goes up:   r × exp(+σ × √Δt)
  goes down: r × exp(−σ × √Δt)

Probability of each: 0.5 (risk-neutral measure).

"Recombining" means up-then-down arrives at the same rate as down-then-up. This keeps the tree from exploding exponentially — after N steps you have N+1 nodes, not 2^N.

def build_rate_tree(short_rate, volatility, steps, dt=1.0):
    u = np.exp(volatility * np.sqrt(dt))
    d = 1.0 / u
    tree = []
    for i in range(steps + 1):
        level = []
        for j in range(i + 1):
            rate = short_rate * (u ** (i - 2 * j))
            level.append(round(rate, 6))
        tree.append(level)
    return tree

With 5% short rate and 20% volatility, the 5-step tree fans out from 5.0% at the root to a range of roughly 2.7%–9.2% at the leaves. Higher volatility means a wider range of rate outcomes, which makes the call option more valuable (more scenarios where the issuer wants to refinance).

Backward Induction: Pricing on the Tree

Bond pricing on the tree works backwards from maturity:

  1. At maturity (leaf nodes): value = face + final coupon.
  2. At each earlier node: the continuation value is the discounted expected value of the two successor nodes, plus any coupon:
continuation = (0.5 × V_up + 0.5 × V_down) / (1 + r × Δt) + coupon

For a callable bond, the issuer compares continuation value to the call price at each node. If calling is cheaper, they call:

value = min(continuation, call_price + coupon)

This is where the option bites. In low-rate nodes (where the bond is worth more than par to the holder), the issuer calls. The bondholder's upside is capped. The result: the callable bond always prices at or below the non-callable.

Z-Spread: The Flat Approach

Z-spread ignores optionality entirely. It finds the constant spread z such that discounting all cash flows off the spot curve plus z matches the market price:

P = ∑ CF_i / (1 + s_i + z)^t_i = market_price

Solved numerically (Brent's method in our case):

def calculate_z_spread(spot_curve, coupon, maturity_years, face, market_price):
    # Interpolate spot curve, set up cash flows...
    def objective(z_bps):
        z = z_bps / 10000.0
        price = sum(cf / (1 + s + z) ** t for cf, s, t in zip(cfs, spots, times))
        return price - market_price

    return round(brentq(objective, -500, 3000), 2)

For a 5% coupon, 5-year bond priced at 98 on a flat 5% curve, the Z-spread comes out to about 47 bps.

OAS: Through the Tree

OAS does the same thing — find the spread that equates model price to market price — but on the binomial tree. The spread is added to every rate at every node. For callable bonds, the call decision happens after the OAS shift, so the option value is baked into the model price rather than absorbed into the spread.

def calculate_oas(spot_curve, coupon, maturity_years, face,
                  call_price, market_price, volatility=0.15):
    tree = build_rate_tree(short_rate, volatility, maturity_years)

    def objective(oas_bps):
        if call_price is not None:
            price = price_callable_bond_on_tree(tree, coupon, face, call_price,
                                                 oas_bps=oas_bps)
        else:
            shifted_tree = [[r + oas_bps/100 for r in lvl] for lvl in tree]
            price = price_bond_on_tree(shifted_tree, coupon, face)
        return price - market_price

    return round(brentq(objective, -500, 3000), 2)

For the same bond with a call at par: OAS comes in significantly below the Z-spread. The difference is the option cost — what the bondholder gives up for the issuer's right to call.

Why OAS and Z-spread don't match exactly for non-callable bonds

In theory, OAS should equal Z-spread when there's no option. In practice, with a binomial tree at nonzero volatility, the rate distribution introduces convexity effects that flat discounting doesn't capture. At 20% vol, we see about a 15 bps gap. At 0% vol the tree collapses to flat discounting and the two converge. This is a real model effect, not a bug.
